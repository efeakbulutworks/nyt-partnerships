Partnership Types & Pipeline

Not all partnerships are created equal — and they shouldn't be managed the same way. A consistent typology makes it possible to staff, sequence, and forecast the portfolio without relitigating every deal from scratch. Below are the four partnership types I'd use to organize the NYT book, followed by a sample pipeline view.

Flagship Partnerships

Marquee, high-investment relationships that define what NYT partnership can look like at its best. These are the deals Dao and the team point to as proof of concept — category exclusives, multi-year commitments, full-ecosystem activations. They take the most planning, require the most cross-functional coordination, and carry the greatest reputational stakes. The portfolio should carry a small number of these at any time — enough to anchor the book, not so many they drain the team's capacity for everything else.

  • Multi-year, category-exclusive partner arrangements
  • Full-ecosystem activations across NYT, The Athletic, Cooking, Games, Wirecutter
  • Custom editorial integrations with original content development
  • Major tentpole event sponsorships (e.g., NYT DealBook, The Athletic championships coverage)

Scalable Recurring Partnerships

The engine of the portfolio. These are partnerships with defined, repeatable structures — newsletter sponsorships, podcast integrations, seasonal content programs — that can be templated, executed efficiently, and renewed without starting from zero. The goal here is to build a library of modular frameworks (see Section 07) that make these deals faster to close and cheaper to execute without compromising the partner experience.

  • Newsletter sponsorship packages (daily, weekly, thematic)
  • Podcast host-read and mid-roll integrations
  • Seasonal and recurring cooking, games, or Wirecutter content programs
  • Athletic season-long presenting partnerships

Cultural Moments

Time-bound partnerships tied to a specific cultural event, season, or news cycle. These have a clear start and end, and their value is often in timeliness and association rather than long-term integration. They require surge capacity — the ability to move fast — but don't demand the same multi-month planning runway as flagship deals. The pitch here is relevance: a partner aligned with a major news moment or cultural beat gets audience attention that money alone can't buy.

  • Election cycle partnerships and political advertising integrations
  • Olympics, World Cup, and major sports event co-sponsorships (Athletic)
  • Holiday and seasonal branded content programs (Cooking, Wirecutter gift guides)
  • Breaking news or cultural moment fast-turn activations

Experimental & Standalone

This bucket is intentional — and important. Not every interesting idea fits a template. Experimental partnerships are how NYT tests new models, formats, and categories before they become standard offerings. The discipline is to run these as bounded pilots: defined scope, clear success metrics, and an explicit decision point at the end (scale it, template it, or sunset it). Without that discipline, experimental becomes a euphemism for under-resourced and over-promised.

  • New format pilots (immersive audio, AR/VR, shoppable editorial)
  • Category entries (health, finance, travel) where partnership models are still being defined
  • Platform-native experiments (social, streaming, live)
  • International partnership tests outside the US market

Modular Partnership Architecture

The tension in premium partnership work is real: partners want bespoke, but bespoke doesn't scale. Every deal built from scratch taxes the team, stretches timelines, and makes it harder to forecast capacity. The answer isn't to lower standards — it's to separate the layers.

Standardize the backend. Bespoke the frontend.

A modular architecture means the team builds a library of proven, reusable building blocks — content formats, distribution mechanisms, measurement frameworks, approval workflows — and then assembles them in partner-specific combinations. The partner experiences something designed for them. The team is working from a tested toolkit, not a blank page.

This is how the BBC's commercial partnerships team operated at scale: a defined menu of partnership components that could be combined, sequenced, and priced flexibly, without requiring bespoke production on every engagement. The result was faster closes, cleaner delivery, and stronger renewal rates — because both sides knew what they were getting.

The Building Blocks

Each module is a discrete, tested component with a defined scope, production cost, and delivery timeline. Partnerships are assembled from combinations of modules. A flagship deal might use eight modules across three brands. A recurring newsletter deal might use two.

Content Formats

Branded editorial, sponsored newsletters, native articles, recipe integrations, puzzle activations, Wirecutter-style reviews, podcast segments, video series.

Distribution Channels

Homepage, section fronts, email newsletters, push notifications, social amplification, podcast feed, app placements, search integration.

Event & Experiential

Presenting sponsorships, hosted dinner or salon, press event integration, live panel or broadcast co-production, awards sponsorship.

Data & Intelligence

Proprietary audience insight reports, co-branded research, category trend analysis using NYT subscriber and behavioral data.

Audience Access

Subscriber co-marketing, lookalike targeting, contextual category exclusivity, first-look audience access for new products or services.

Measurement & Attribution

Brand lift studies, attention metrics, subscription influence modeling, e-commerce attribution, cross-brand reach and frequency reporting.

Cross-Brand Bundles

Pre-packaged multi-property combinations (e.g., NYT + Athletic sports package; Cooking + Wirecutter home & lifestyle bundle) with unified reporting.

Creative Services

T Brand Studio integration, custom visual identity development, branded content production, partner-facing creative briefing and approval workflows.

Renewal & Expansion

Defined renewal review cadence, expansion trigger criteria, performance benchmarks, multi-year pricing structures, category exclusivity renewal terms.

How It Works in Practice

When a new partner conversation begins, the team isn't starting from scratch — they're consulting the module library and scoping which combination fits the partner's goals, budget, and timeline. This changes the sales conversation: instead of "what would you like?", it becomes "here are the three partnership constructs that fit your objectives — let's choose and customize."

The architecture also makes resource planning tractable. Each module has a known production cost, a known lead time, and a known team requirement. When the portfolio manager looks at the Q3 pipeline, they can estimate staffing needs against the module composition of each deal in progress — before those deals close.

What This Unlocks

  • Faster time-to-close: Partners see structured options rather than an open brief; decisions are easier and faster.
  • Cleaner delivery: Teams execute against known templates, not one-off production specs.
  • Better renewal rates: Partners who understood what they bought are more likely to renew and expand.
  • Portfolio forecasting: Module-level costing enables real capacity planning across the team.
  • Equity across brands: Every NYT property benefits from the same modular toolkit — Cooking and Games partnerships get the same operational discipline as NYT flagship deals.

Why This Excites Me

The straightforward answer is the one I'd give in any interview: NYT is the most credible journalism organization in the world, the partnership opportunity is genuinely complex, and the role has real strategic scope. All true. But that's the polished version. Here's the more honest one.

The BBC Parallel

I spent years at the BBC working on commercial partnerships in an environment that had most of the same structural tensions NYT has now: world-class editorial with deep audience trust, a commercial operation trying to monetize that trust without degrading it, multiple premium brands under one roof, and the constant pressure to scale without losing what made it worth scaling.

The BBC's answer — at least in the commercial partnerships work I was part of — was to build operating discipline that respected the editorial firewall while still delivering commercial value at scale. That meant clear frameworks, modular approaches, and a team culture that was proud of the quality bar, not resentful of it.

When I read the job description for this role and thought about what I'd heard about where NYT's partnership operation is, I saw the same inflection point. The demand is there. The brands are extraordinary. The question is how you build the operating layer that lets you say yes to the right things, faster, without burning the team or diluting the product.

That is the exact problem I want to work on.

The Multi-Brand Opportunity

What NYT has that the BBC didn't — at least not in the same way — is a genuinely differentiated multi-brand portfolio where the brands actually mean something distinct to distinct audiences. The Athletic isn't a poor man's NYT Sports section. Cooking isn't a recipe appendix. Wirecutter built its reputation on editorial independence from commercial pressure. Games has a daily active user base with remarkable engagement metrics.

That portfolio is a commercial asset that is, I think, still being discovered. Not because the team isn't talented — clearly they are — but because the frameworks for cross-brand partnership development at this scale are still being invented. The JetBlue case I describe in the About section is one small data point in that direction: what happens when you build a partnership that draws on multiple properties' strengths simultaneously, with a coherent story for the partner about why the combination is worth more than the parts.

What I'd Bring

Operating experience in premium multi-brand environments. A genuine affection for the editorial product — I am, embarrassingly, a subscriber to four of the five NYT properties. A bias toward frameworks and tools that help teams make faster, better decisions rather than frameworks that create bureaucracy. And a track record of building partner relationships that renew, because the first deal was delivered clearly and the partner understood what they got.

I'm not coming in with the answer. I'm coming in with the right questions and enough experience to build the answer alongside Dao and the team.

The demand is there. The brands are extraordinary. The question is how you build the operating layer.

The JetBlue Case

The partnership I'm most proud of — and most instructive for the NYT role — is a multi-brand content and distribution partnership I developed with JetBlue. The core insight was that JetBlue didn't need another advertising placement. They needed a way to tell a coherent story about travel, lifestyle, and discovery to an audience that was both aspirational and actually traveling.

The solution was a partnership that drew on multiple content properties simultaneously: travel-oriented editorial, food and restaurant content, destination-specific programming, and audience targeting that mapped to JetBlue's route network. The deal was structured modularly — each component had a clear scope and deliverable — which made it possible to expand in year two without renegotiating the entire relationship from scratch.

The outcome: a multi-year renewal, an expanded scope, and a case study that became a template for how to pitch cross-brand partnerships to travel and hospitality categories going forward.
