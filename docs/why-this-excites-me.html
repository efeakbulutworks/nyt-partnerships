Section 06 · The New York Times Advertising Partnerships

Why This Excites Me

The straightforward answer is the one I'd give in any interview: NYT is the most credible journalism organization in the world, the partnership opportunity is genuinely complex, and the role has real strategic scope. All true. But that's the polished version. Here's the more honest one.

The BBC Parallel

I spent years at the BBC working on commercial partnerships in an environment that had most of the same structural tensions NYT has now: world-class editorial with deep audience trust, a commercial operation trying to monetize that trust without degrading it, multiple premium brands under one roof, and the constant pressure to scale without losing what made it worth scaling.

The BBC's answer — at least in the commercial partnerships work I was part of — was to build operating discipline that respected the editorial firewall while still delivering commercial value at scale. That meant clear frameworks, modular approaches, and a team culture that was proud of the quality bar, not resentful of it.

When I read the job description for this role and thought about what I'd heard about where NYT's partnership operation is, I saw the same inflection point. The demand is there. The brands are extraordinary. The question is how you build the operating layer that lets you say yes to the right things, faster, without burning the team or diluting the product.

That is the exact problem I want to work on.

The Multi-Brand Opportunity

What NYT has that the BBC didn't — at least not in the same way — is a genuinely differentiated multi-brand portfolio where the brands actually mean something distinct to distinct audiences. The Athletic isn't a poor man's NYT Sports section. Cooking isn't a recipe appendix. Wirecutter built its reputation on editorial independence from commercial pressure. Games has a daily active user base with remarkable engagement metrics.

That portfolio is a commercial asset that is, I think, still being discovered. Not because the team isn't talented — clearly they are — but because the frameworks for cross-brand partnership development at this scale are still being invented. The JetBlue case I describe in the About section is one small data point in that direction: what happens when you build a partnership that draws on multiple properties' strengths simultaneously, with a coherent story for the partner about why the combination is worth more than the parts.

What I'd Bring

Operating experience in premium multi-brand environments. A genuine affection for the editorial product — I am, embarrassingly, a subscriber to four of the five NYT properties. A bias toward frameworks and tools that help teams make faster, better decisions rather than frameworks that create bureaucracy. And a track record of building partner relationships that renew, because the first deal was delivered clearly and the partner understood what they got.

I'm not coming in with the answer. I'm coming in with the right questions and enough experience to build the answer alongside Dao and the team.

The demand is there. The brands are extraordinary. The question is how you build the operating layer.
