Section 04 · The New York Times Advertising Partnerships

Partnership Types & Pipeline

Not all partnerships are created equal — and they shouldn't be managed the same way. A consistent typology makes it possible to staff, sequence, and forecast the portfolio without relitigating every deal from scratch. Below are the four partnership types I'd use to organize the NYT book, followed by a sample pipeline view.

Flagship Partnerships

Marquee, high-investment relationships that define what NYT partnership can look like at its best. These are the deals Dao and the team point to as proof of concept — category exclusives, multi-year commitments, full-ecosystem activations. They take the most planning, require the most cross-functional coordination, and carry the greatest reputational stakes. The portfolio should carry a small number of these at any time — enough to anchor the book, not so many they drain the team's capacity for everything else.

  • Multi-year, category-exclusive partner arrangements
  • Full-ecosystem activations across NYT, The Athletic, Cooking, Games, Wirecutter
  • Custom editorial integrations with original content development
  • Major tentpole event sponsorships (e.g., NYT DealBook, The Athletic championships coverage)

Scalable Recurring Partnerships

The engine of the portfolio. These are partnerships with defined, repeatable structures — newsletter sponsorships, podcast integrations, seasonal content programs — that can be templated, executed efficiently, and renewed without starting from zero. The goal here is to build a library of modular frameworks (see Section 05) that make these deals faster to close and cheaper to execute without compromising the partner experience.

  • Newsletter sponsorship packages (daily, weekly, thematic)
  • Podcast host-read and mid-roll integrations
  • Seasonal and recurring cooking, games, or Wirecutter content programs
  • Athletic season-long presenting partnerships

Cultural Moments

Time-bound partnerships tied to a specific cultural event, season, or news cycle. These have a clear start and end, and their value is often in timeliness and association rather than long-term integration. They require surge capacity — the ability to move fast — but don't demand the same multi-month planning runway as flagship deals. The pitch here is relevance: a partner aligned with a major news moment or cultural beat gets audience attention that money alone can't buy.

  • Election cycle partnerships and political advertising integrations
  • Olympics, World Cup, and major sports event co-sponsorships (Athletic)
  • Holiday and seasonal branded content programs (Cooking, Wirecutter gift guides)
  • Breaking news or cultural moment fast-turn activations

Experimental & Standalone

This bucket is intentional — and important. Not every interesting idea fits a template. Experimental partnerships are how NYT tests new models, formats, and categories before they become standard offerings. The discipline is to run these as bounded pilots: defined scope, clear success metrics, and an explicit decision point at the end (scale it, template it, or sunset it). Without that discipline, experimental becomes a euphemism for under-resourced and over-promised.

  • New format pilots (immersive audio, AR/VR, shoppable editorial)
  • Category entries (health, finance, travel) where partnership models are still being defined
  • Platform-native experiments (social, streaming, live)
  • International partnership tests outside the US market
