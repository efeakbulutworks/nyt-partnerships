The JetBlue Case

The partnership I'm most proud of — and most instructive for the NYT role — is a multi-brand content and distribution partnership I developed with JetBlue. The core insight was that JetBlue didn't need another advertising placement. They needed a way to tell a coherent story about travel, lifestyle, and discovery to an audience that was both aspirational and actually traveling.

The solution was a partnership that drew on multiple content properties simultaneously: travel-oriented editorial, food and restaurant content, destination-specific programming, and audience targeting that mapped to JetBlue's route network. The deal was structured modularly — each component had a clear scope and deliverable — which made it possible to expand in year two without renegotiating the entire relationship from scratch.

The outcome: a multi-year renewal, an expanded scope, and a case study that became a template for how to pitch cross-brand partnerships to travel and hospitality categories going forward.

Why The Times Specifically

I've been deliberate about where I've invested my career, and I'm being deliberate here. The New York Times is not just the biggest opportunity in media partnerships right now — it's the most interesting. The combination of editorial ambition, brand portfolio complexity, audience depth, and the specific inflection point the partnerships operation is at makes this a once-in-a-career problem to work on.

I also think the work matters beyond the commercial outcome. NYT's ability to fund its journalism depends, in part, on the health and growth of its commercial operations. That's a relationship I take seriously. The best partnership work I've done has always been in service of something larger than the deal itself — and this role has that quality in abundance.

I want to build something here. Not just execute a pipeline. Build the operating model, the frameworks, the team culture, and the partner relationships that compound over time. That's the pitch.
